(a) Explain why global rice prices are expected to increase sharply. [10]

Common mistake 1: Listing factors without market analysis

Do not merely say “population rises so prices rise”. Explain that demand increases, demand curve shifts right, shortage occurs at the original price, and price rises to clear the shortage.

Common mistake 2: Forgetting why the increase is sharp

The word sharply requires PED and PES analysis. Explain that rice has price-inelastic demand and supply, so a large price increase is needed to clear the shortage.

Common mistake 3: Weak context

Use rice context: staple food, necessity, Asian consumers, animal feed, bad weather, crop yields, agricultural gestation period, China and India if relevant.

Common mistake 4: Invalid equilibrium link

Do not claim price rises simply because “demand is higher”. The valid link is through shortage, upward pressure on price, and movement to a new equilibrium.

Common mistake 5: Missing combined impact

A strong answer combines both a rightward shift of demand and a leftward shift of supply, leading to a larger shortage and sharper rise in equilibrium price.

Introduction When a market is in equilibrium, there is no tendency for price and quantity to change unless external forces act on the market. In the global rice market, changes in both demand and supply, together with the price-inelastic nature of demand and supply for rice, explain why global rice prices are expected to increase sharply. Demand factors Global rice demand has risen due to an increase in world consumption, possibly caused by population growth and rising income. Since rice is a normal good, higher household income may cause consumers to demand more rice. This is especially relevant in large emerging economies where population size and purchasing power have been rising. There is also greater use of rice for animal feeding. Rice is used as a factor input in animal rearing. As demand for meat rises, farmers require more rice to feed animals, increasing the derived demand for rice. Hence, global demand for rice rises, shifting the demand curve rightward from D1 to D2. Supply factors At the same time, global rice supply has fallen due to lower crop yields in major rice-producing countries. Bad weather conditions may reduce harvests and lower land productivity. This decreases the quantity of rice supplied at each price, shifting the supply curve leftward from S1 to S2. PED analysis Demand for rice is price inelastic because rice is a necessity and a staple food for many consumers, especially in Asia. When price rises, quantity demanded falls less than proportionately, ceteris paribus. Hence, a large increase in price is needed to reduce quantity demanded enough to clear the shortage. PES analysis Supply of rice is also price inelastic in the short run because agricultural production has a long gestation period. Rice producers cannot immediately increase output in response to higher prices. Hence, quantity supplied rises less than proportionately when price increases, ceteris paribus. A large increase in price is therefore needed for the market to reach a new equilibrium. Combined impact With reference to the demand and supply diagram, the increase in demand from D1 to D2 and the decrease in supply from S1 to S2 create a large shortage at the original equilibrium price P1. This causes strong upward pressure on price, leading to a sharp rise in global rice prices from P1 to P2. If the increase in demand is larger than the fall in supply, equilibrium quantity may rise slightly from Q1 to Q2. Conclusion Therefore, global rice prices are expected to rise sharply due to the combined effect of rising demand, falling supply, and the price-inelastic nature of both demand and supply for rice.
